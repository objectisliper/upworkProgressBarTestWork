An attacker may enter company premises and pretend to be an employee, visitor, or service personnel. They may be dressed in appropriate attire or impersonate someone from the contract cleaning crew.

 

In the classic con, an attacker poses as someone in your company, a consultant (e.g., tech support – complete with fabricated ID card and clipboard), or another trusted outside authority such as an auditor.

VISITORS: “ENTERING THE OFFICE WITH A TRICK”

Would you hold a visitor’s door open if he had both hands full?

While the person asking may not seem suspicious, this is a very common tactic used by social engineers.

"Can you hold the door for me? I don't have my key/access card on me"

LESSON 1: SOCIAL ENGINEERING / BAITING

Cards  
Points  

Susan John explains that baiting closely resembles phishing attacks.

What distinguishes baiting from other types of social engineering is the promise of an item or good that hackers use to entice their victims.

Baiters may offer free music or movie downloads in exchange for the user’s login credentials to a certain site.

Baiting attacks are not restricted to online schemes, either. Attackers can deftly exploit human curiosity via physical media. The baiting attack begins when the malicious actor leaves a malware-infected portable device, such as a USB flash drive or CD, in a place where someone is likely to find it.

The success of any baiting attack hinges on the notion that the person who finds the device will load it into their computer and unknowingly install the malware without any user interaction.

In the case of “Bad USB,” the computer is deceived that a keyboard or similar device is being plugged in. Anyone who has ever plugged in a new keyboard will have noticed that the computer tries to install it automatically. A small hourglass appears, then a message pops up notifying that the keyboard can now be used. The “Bad USB” uses this automatic installation process to infect the computer.

Once installed, the malware allows the attacker to advance into the victim’s system.

A new study has shown that almost half of all people who find a USB stick in a parking lot will connect it to their PC. If you find a random USB drive lying around, don’t touch it!

Points  

WHAT IS “SHOULDER SURFING”?

Shoulder surfing is the strategy of using techniques of direct observation, such as looking over someone's shoulder, to acquire information.

WHAT IS THE RISK?

Whether you're on a plane, a train, or at a restaurant, you've no doubt noticed curious neighbors staring at your laptop screen. In fact, according to studies, most mobile workers have already experienced the obtrusive glances of "shoulder surfers." Maybe people just look out of curiosity. But have you ever thought that these people could also be competitors or thieves trying to steal sensitive information without your consent? The biggest problem these days is that you don't even have to stand behind a person to steal their sensitive information.

Hackers only need to take advantage of modern surveillance technology – since security cameras are used in most public places, all they have to do is access them. And some security systems don't even need to be hacked; anyone can access them with their default username and password. We all have data stored on our devices that no one else should see. It can be sensitive financial information, research results from top secret marketing plans, or legally protected emails.

Every year, companies spend millions of dollars on cyber-security software, services, and hardware to prevent the theft of confidential data. But this costly technological effort is useless if someone can take a quick and secret photo of your unprotected screen. Shoulder surfers are at best annoying but can cause more serious consequences depending on the data they collect. For example, under the new General Data Protection Rules (GDPR) passed in the European Union, a company could be penalized for disclosing the personal data of EU citizens. In the US, the disclosure of personal medical records by healthcare executives or physicians could be considered a legal violation.

HOW TO DISCOURAGE SHOULDER SURFING?

One of the best protective measures against this type of attack is a privacy screen, which prevents unwanted viewing. The Privacy Filters work with black-out technology, so that the screen appears black when viewed at an angle. If you're sitting in the middle seat of an airplane, Internet café, or hotel lobby, your screen will appear to be off.
