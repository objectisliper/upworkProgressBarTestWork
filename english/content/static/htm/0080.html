WHAT IS PHISHING?

Phishing is the fraudulent use of electronic communication channels with the purpose of deceiving and taking advantage of unsuspecting users. Via phone, email, or text message, attackers attempt to gain sensitive or confidential information such as usernames, passwords, credit card information, network credentials, and more by posing as a legitimate individual or institution. The information is then used to access important accounts and can result in identity theft and financial loss.

Smishing

Smishing is a very simple but profitable form of phishing conducted via SMS. A popular scenario describes receiving an SMS from an unfamiliar person inviting you to connect online, using a link to their profile page on a social network. Of course, the link is phished, and typing your credentials in the provided fields will steal your login information. Another popular smishing scenario is the “problem with your banking account or credit card. ” In this case, the link goes to a phished bank page.

Although smishing scenarios can vary, the pattern is always the same: luring users into clicking a link where they are asked to input their credentials.

Additional attack options include:

  • Premium SMS: Replying to the message subscribes the victim to a paid service.
  • App installation: Depending on the OS version & type, the user can be tricked into downloading apps that take control of their phone.

Real life example

A typical SMS-based attack happened at the end of 2018, when criminals used SMS phishing to rob cardless ATMs.

Vishing (voice pishing)

This type of phishing might well be the oldest one – deceiving users to extract secret data via phone dates back to the 1990s. The point is simple: scammers call you pretending to represent your bank and try to obtain your account and card data with cunning questions. Nowadays, vishing has stepped up a notch, using not only human scammers but bots and robots as well. This is a real technical leap for cybercriminals because it lets them attack a huge number of potential victims with almost no effort, 24/7. Today, bots can impersonate humans very plausibly thanks to the continuous progress in ML and AI technologies.

Real life example

Multiple cases of fraudulent communication have been observed wherein the adversary claims to represent the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) requesting personal information. In many cases, threatening or coercive language is used to scare individuals into paying fictitious debt to the adversaries’ account (which the attacker forges to look like a CRA account).

Attachment-based phishing

Many users encounter phishing as a link in an attached file. Very often it’s a .pdf that contains nothing else but the malicious link. Clicking on it takes you to a phished webpage that will try to lure out your login credentials.

Nowadays, many people are aware that a .pdf can bring about trouble. But what about other files, for example, .eml voice records? Can they be dangerous?

Just imagine receiving an .eml file with a message that says, “New voice record received.” Of course, you want to hear the message, so you open the file and land on a Microsoft login page which prompts you to enter your credentials.

Many people find it logical as Microsoft takes care of their security. Unfortunately, their credentials fall right into the cybercriminal’s hands. The attachment could also be an HTML file disguised as a web-form from your bank that you’re required to fill in immediately. In reality, the file contains a phishing script meant to extract your credentials once you enter them in the login fields. In all cases, the impact is the same – the cybercriminals get your credentials.

Real life example

One phishing campaign that was underway in 2019 sent out files disguised as a voicemail received through RingCentral.

Spear phishing

Spear-phishing has come to be known as “the main email attachment threat.” To make the attack more believable, criminals use information that applies specifically to you. The email can even appear to come from someone you know. To obtain the information they need for these types of attacks, hackers dig around social media sites, blogs, forums, etc. For example, share online that you will be traveling to New York soon, and you might receive an email from what appears to be a colleague, saying, “Hey, while you’re in New York you’ve got to eat at Restaurant X.” There will also be a convenient link that will claim to take you to the restaurant’s website. However, clicking on it will niftily infect your computer with malware – all while you’re studying the menu.

PHISHING: Understanding Domains

A "domain name" is the name of the location on the Internet that holds the website you are visiting. The domain name in the example below is "example-company.com".

http://www.example-company.com/webfolder/page.html

The domain name is followed by the name of a folder called "webfolder". In this folder, you find a web page called "page.html" (far right end of the URL). Those sections of the URL are separated by forward slashes. The person running the website can name the sections following the domain name however they choose. The "URL" is the complete Internet/web address for a web page. A URL can contain many forward slashes and other punctuation, but the second and third slashes always enclose the domain name. The actual domain is found at the right end of that section, just before the 3rd slash mark:

http://www.example-company.com/

We could make various additions to the “example-company.com” URL, like adding "google.com," and then the URL might look like this:

http://google.com.example-company.com/whatever/something-else/article.html

The actual domain is at the right end of that section, between the second slash and just before the 3rd slash mark.

Seeing the above, some people might be fooled into thinking they were at Google.com. But the website visited would still be "example-company.com" as the actual domain always sits at the right end of that section, just before the 3rd slash mark.
