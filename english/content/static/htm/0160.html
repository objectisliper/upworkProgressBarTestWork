EMAIL ATTACKS AGAINST THE USER

  • SPAM: Spam is defined as unsolicited commercial email messages that are sent in bulk. The main risk of not confronting a spam problem is the saturation of your electronic mailbox. Another problem is the huge loss of time incurred by sorting out the legitimate messages sitting among the spam.
  • PHISHING: Somewhat related to spam is phishing. It refers to the use of deceptive computer-based means to trick individuals into responding to an email, opening an attachment, or clicking on a link, all of which lead to disclosing sensitive information. Phishing emails appear to come from organizations or companies you trust, but they're often the gateway to identity theft.
  • SPYWARE & VIRUSES: Spyware may be received as an attachment in an email and, once executed, gives criminals access to your information. Viruses can spread through email to your entire contact list without your permission or knowledge.

UNINTENTIONAL ACTS BY AUTHORIZED USERS

  • EXPOSURE OF CONFIDENTIAL DATA: Authorized users may inadvertently send proprietary or other sensitive information via email, exposing the organization to embarrassment or legal charges.
  • USING A PERSONAL EMAIL FOR BUSINESS: All business-related correspondence must be conducted via the organization's email accounts. The use of external services, such as GMX, Yahoo, Gmail, Hotmail, etc., for business-related purposes, is not permitted.
    Using personal emails is risky as the accounts are outside of the IT department’s control. They are also not subject to backup, archiving, or security. Data that leaves the organization via private email will have no correspondence record or audit trail. Another problem is that personal email may be an avenue through which non-business-related content may enter the corporate network. Although most personal email providers take appropriate actions to screen malware from their systems, such controls may be less stringent than those required for corporate networks.
  • WEAK PASSWORDS: A weak email account password could leave your sensitive information vulnerable. In fact, a weak password is a cyber criminal's best friend, so it's essential to create passwords that effectively protect you and your company.

LESSON 4: EMAIL SECURITY / TIPs

Cards  
Points  
TIP
#1/12

Don’t use a personal account for transmitting company data

Make sure you don’t risk your company’s security by transmitting sensitive company data via your personal computer or email address. This includes forwarding company documents to your personal email account as well as using that account for business-related correspondence.

TIP
#2/12

Don’t disclose confidential information in response to any email

All messages that try to persuade you to divulge your password or credit card number are forged, even if they appear to come from your system administrator or your bank.

TIP
#3/12

Always log off after accessing corporate resources from a public device

Whenever you need to use a public device to complete a job task, remember to log off after you’re done. If you do not, the next person to use the same computer will have free access to your personal or company information.

TIP
#4/12

When applicable, use the blind carbon copy (BCC) option

When you include a person’s email address in the BCC: rather than the CC: field, none of the recipients can see the others’ email addresses.

New email users often rely too much on the TO: field because it is the default way of sending emails. That is fine as long as you are writing to just one person. However, if you are sending mail out to a diverse group of people, confusing BCC: and CC: raises some serious privacy and security concerns. It takes just one spammer to get a hold of the email and immediately everyone on that email list will get spammed.

TIP
#5/12

Don’t be trigger happy with the “Reply All” button

Sometimes the issue doesn’t involve the CC: and BCC: fields and instead concerns hitting “Reply All” instead of “Reply.” When you hit “Reply All,” your email message is sent to everyone on the original email list. However, if you didn’t intend to include all recipients, the information you share might compromise your security and cause personal humiliation.

TIP
#6/12

Spamming as a result of email forwarding

Forwarding emails can be a great way to quickly bring someone up to speed on a subject without having to write up a summary email. However, if you aren’t careful, forwarding emails can create a significant security threat for yourself and all earlier recipients. When forwarding a message, all of its previous recipients (until that point in time) are automatically listed in the body of the email. As the chain keeps expanding, more and more recipient IDs are included in the list.

TIP
#7/12

Don’t think that an erased email is gone forever

Just because you delete an email from your “Inbox” and the sender deletes it from their “Sent” folder, does not mean that the message is purged forever. In fact, deleted emails often still exist in backup folders on remote servers for years and can be retrieved by skilled professionals. So, start to think of emails as permanent documents and be careful about what you put into writing.

TIP
#8/12

Don't click on suspicious links

If a link appears disguised or comes from an unfamiliar source, don’t click on it. It might take you to an unspecified location and possibly inject malware into your machine when you attempt to download the page. Be cautious about shortened links, and pay attention for any discrepancies in links that look mostly (but not entirely) familiar, such as “Amaz0n” instead of “Amazon.”

TIP
#9/12

Be cautious with emails from unknown senders

It’s important to be cautious when an email from an unknown sender is delivered to your inbox. Avoid opening any attachments you aren’t expecting, especially if they seem peculiar or out of context. These types of emails are often malicious.

TIP
#10/12

Don’t believe you won the lottery… and other scam titles

Spammers use a wide variety of clever titles to get you to open emails which they fill with all sorts of bad things. New email users often make the mistake of opening such emails.

TIP
#11/12

Unsubscribing from newsletters you have never subscribed to

A common technique spammers use involves sending out thousands of fake newsletters from different organizations. The feature they make use of is the “unsubscribe” link at the bottom of the newsletter. Email users who decide to unsubscribe from the list actually end up getting loads of spam.

So, if you don’t specifically remember subscribing to the particular newsletter, you are better off just blacklisting the email address.

TIP
#12/12

Don’t always trust emails from friends

Most new Internet users are very careful when it comes to emails sent by people they don’t recognize. However, when a friend sends them an email, all caution goes out the window, as the user assumes their friend wouldn’t intend to hurt them. In truth, an email from a friend’s ID is just as likely to contain a virus or malware as that coming from a stranger’s. The reason is simple: most malware is unknowingly circulated by people who own a device that has been infiltrated by hackers.
