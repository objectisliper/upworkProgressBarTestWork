John Susan explains, that having a password is the most basic protection you can have for the information you store in services or applications, be it your personal Linkedin or Facebook account, your online shopping page or your company's internal customer management system. The problem is that many services now are online and everything needs a password. It is therefore tempting to choose a password that is simple and easy to remember.

Maybe you have a password that you have used for everything since school. Or maybe you write down your password so you don't forget it. If you do one of these things, you are probably in the majority, not the minority. Creating long, complex passwords that are unique to each service you use is a challenge. It's almost impossible to remember them all for all services. But the problem is that simple, easy to remember passwords are also easy to "crack".

The majority of network break-ins (also known as "leaks") are caused by weak or stolen passwords. As soon as attackers have your password, they also have access to your account and all the information stored in it. From there, they may be able to do all sorts of things, and what was meant to be protection could become a threat to you. Learn more about this topic with the cards below.

WHY WOULD ANYONE WANT TO STEAL MY PASSWORD?

  • Passwords are one of the most important keys that prevent unauthorized access to many systems.
  • Hackers can damage your files. For example, in ransomware attacks, cyber criminals charge money so that you can access your data yourself.
  • Hackers can gain access to confidential data that they can use or sell personally.
  • Hackers can send embarrassing or offensive emails that impersonate you.
  • Hackers may need your identity. Your information may be used to perform financial operations on your behalf.
  • Hackers may wish to gain access to computer resources such as bandwidth, processing power or storage space.
  • Hackers can use your PC to attack others.

How hackers get your passwords

Password guessing or dictionary attack

The easiest method used to crack a password is to simply guess. It’s usually quite simple because many people use personal information such as family names, birth dates, 111111, ‘password,’ etc. as their password. More sophisticated hacking methods include using software that can decrypt passwords within minutes by using ‘dictionary’ and ‘brute force’ attacks.

A hacker may launch a dictionary attack by passing every word through a dictionary, which can contain foreign languages in addition to the entire English language, to a login program hoping that a word will eventually match the correct password.

Password reuse

Many people use the same username and password for all their accounts. Hackers run programs that enter stolen usernames and passwords on tens of thousands of websites until they encounter the same password. Then they have access to your accounts and credentials. If you use the same username and password for all accounts, it can be extremely dangerous.

But how do hackers get access to your passwords? One possibility are websites where you may have registered. Sometimes those sites are hacked and their passwords are leaked. Hackers use these leaks to test whether the passwords work on other websites.

Social Engineering

Most hackers use social engineering skills where for example they send a message via email, sms or instant messenger saying, "This person has posted your bad picture on Facebook, click here to check your photo". Once you click on the link, you will be taken to the login page and once you enter your password, it will be available to the hacker. Instead of using email, the hacker could use also the phone and pretend he is from support, simply asking you for your password.

ATTACKS VIA WI-FI

  • Wi-Fi traffic monitoring: Have you ever connected to a public Wi-Fi and logged into an account? Then your password may have already been stolen. A common attack is monitoring Wi-Fi traffic, where a hacker uses a simple application that can be easily downloaded from the Internet to monitor all traffic on a public Wi-Fi network.
  • Rogue WI-FI Access Point: A Rogue WI-FI Access Point is basically an unauthorized access point. For example, an attacker inside an airport could set up an access point called "Airport - Free WIFI" and trick users into connecting to their WI-FI network. Since all connections to remote services are made via the hacker's access point, it is only a matter of time before the hacker gains access to your passwords.

TWO-FACTOR AUTHENTICATION (2FA)

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA), sometimes referred to as two-factor verification or two-factor authentication, is a security process in which the user provides two different authentication elements to verify themselves and better protect both the user's credentials and the resources the user can access. Two-Factor Authentication is actually nothing new. If you’ve ever made a purchase via credit card and you’ve been asked to also enter your zip code, that’s 2FA at work. You’ve provided a second set of known information to finalize your transaction.

Two-factor authentication adds an additional layer of security to the authentication process by making it more difficult for hackers to gain access to a person's devices or online accounts, because knowing the victim's password alone is not enough to pass the verification.

PASSWORD MANAGER

One of the most important steps you can take to protect yourself online is to use a unique, secure password for each of your accounts and apps. Unfortunately, it's most likely impossible for you to remember all your different passwords for all your different accounts. That's why so many people use the same password again. A simple solution is to use a password manager, sometimes called a password vault. Password managers store your login information for all the websites and services you use and help you log into them automatically. They encrypt your password database with a master password - the master password is the only one you have to remember. Many password managers can also generate secure passwords that you can then use on your websites. This way you only have to remember one password: the one for the tool. When you log in to new systems, the password manager should also be able to capture this new credential and ask you if you want to save it.

It is essential that the master password you use to protect the contents of the Password Manager is strong and very hard for others to guess. Finally, you should remember your master password. If you forget it, you won't be able to access any of your other passwords.

Here are a few Password Security TIPs

TIP
#1

Don’t use the same password over and over again

Passwords should be changed on a regular basis, at least once every 180 days.
The primary reason is that if someone has cracked your password without you being aware of it, it makes them have to start all over again.
A strong password is never reused.

TIP
#2

Categorize your passwords

Categorize your passwords in order to minimize the number of passwords you have to remember but also to provide a barrier between systems with sensitive information and those with non-sensitive information. For example, you can use the same password for Gmail and Hotmail, but you should not use the same password for your online banking program.
The password you have for your Hotmail (or other Internet email services) should be different than the passwords you use on any work-related systems.

TIP
#3

Change passwords often

It is important to not reuse passwords and change your passwords on a regular basis.

TIP
#4

Avoid using words found in a dictionary

Instead of people guessing passwords, now computers are guessing passwords.
For this reason, if your password is made up of words found in a dictionary, it is very easy for a computer to guess it and gain access to your account.

TIP
#5

Make passwords long

8 characters long or longer is suggested.

TIP
#6

Make passwords complex

Use uppercase, lowercase, special characters, and numbers.

TIP
#7

Don’t re-use passwords

Try not to re-use one of your last five passwords in any form of iteration (e.g. mypassword_2017, maypassword2018 etc.)

TIP
#8

Make passwords non-personal

Avoid using things like your name, birthday, current year, current season, address, phone number, pet’s name, or other information that may be on social networking profiles, public records, or otherwise easily found or guessed.

TIP
#9

Watch where you store passwords

If you absolutely need to write down a password, never store it in obvious places, such as address books, Rolodex files, under drawers or keyboards, or behind pictures.
The worst, but all too common location, is a Post-it note near the computer. Better locations are a safety deposit box or a locked file cabinet. Software is available for popular hand-held computers that can store passwords for numerous accounts in encrypted form.

TIP
#10

Be cautious when using public PC’s

Public computers may not always be securely configured pose a threat to your privacy by storing your password or web cookies. Think twice about going to a secure site if you cannot verify the security of the computer.

TIP
#11

Be cautious when asked for passwords

Don't forget that getting passwords by manipulation of users is an example of social engineering.
An attacker might telephone a user and say, "Hi. OIT here. We're doing a security test. Can we have your password so we can proceed?" Know that any reputable company you do business with will ever ask for your password, and rarely, if ever, need to know your password in order to perform the work.
